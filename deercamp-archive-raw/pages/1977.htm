Neilsville Wisconsin

1977 - p.1.

Eric’s First Buck and Ross’ Last

Well, this was a season of highs and lows.  I shot my first buck and my Dad died during deer season.  I borrowed a 30-30-lever action rifle with a scope from Pete Arnold, a high school friend.  I was sitting on a fold-up chair on the ground, next to the horizontal birch tree.  Gene had left during the summer to join the Air Force so he wasn’t with us that season. 

Anyway, I always seemed to have the most deer action before 9 a.m. and all at once a group of several deer came running by and stopped out in front of me about 50 yards.  I was able to spot a buck right away and watched closely.  Within a minute or so the does ran off, but the buck stayed behind.  It hung back behind some brush and I put the scope on an opening in front of the buck waiting for it to walk out.  To my surprise it did and when I pulled the trigger it dropped in its tracks.  Once anyone had a deer “down” the signal was to make three evenly spaced “toots” on the whistle which meant, “ come here, I have one down.”  One whistle meant, “where are you?” (only to be used if one was lost) and two whistles meant, “come here, I’ve wounded one.”  While I was standing over my buck and blowing three whistles about every 5 minutes, another buck came by and ran off after it saw me.  What I did not know was that right around the time I was shooting my buck, Dad was also shooting at a buck.  Mine was an eight pointer and his was a six or seven pointer.  So all the time I was frantically blowing my whistle and expecting Dad to come running he was looking around for another hunter to watch his buck while he came to help me.  I don’t remember if Dad gutted out the buck for me or talked me through it, but he did do the traditional “swipe” of blood across the forehead.
