Abbotsford, WI

2001 - p.10.

November 17 - Opening Day

from the Northwest.  I swiveled around on my seat to finally see three deer coming my way.  One was a doe, which came within about 30 yards of me.  The other two deer stood behind some brush at about 50 yards out, so I could not see if either was a buck.  After a few seconds, the pair split up with one of the deer with its neck stretched out and nose pointed towards the ground and running towards the first doe.  I saw horns, its a buck!  It stopped by the doe behind some brush, with only from the shoulder backward being visible to shoot at.  Not knowing if I would get a better shot opportunity, I put the crosshairs of the scope on the buck's chest and fire.  The doe ran off immediately and the buck jumped, turned around and stood behind more brush.  It looked like the buck did not know where the shot came from, so I tried another shot at it between some trees.  That one missed too!  After running a short ways, the buck stopped a third time, this time offering me a clear shot to the front shoulder which hit the mark.  The buck dropped with this shot.  Yes!

As I got down from my stand and walked over to the deer, it turned out to be a 5-point buck.  It could have been a six pointer, but the brow tine was broken off.  This was my first buck (and deer) since 1997 when I shot a spike buck back in the school forest.  I then started the task of gutting out the deer and soon Lorin came over.  After congratulations, Lorin took some pictures and then went back to his stand.  I saved the heart for Duane (who likes to cook it on the grill) and also cut off and hung up the buck's sex organs, a tradition to show that a buck had been killed at that spot.  When I was done I walked a short distance away to a water-filled low spot behind my stand to wash my hands.  I was probably back in my stand and ready to hunt again by 2:45 p.m.  Well, the after noon seemed to just crawl by as I was anxious for the end of the day so I could
