Neilsville Wisconsin

1978 - 1983 -  p.3.

Learning to Hunt on My Own

The day was cold so I had my big leather snowmobile chopper mittens on while holding my rifle.  I was facing south when I heard deer running.  I quickly dropped my right hand down so I could pinch my shooting glove between my legs so that I could get my trigger finger ready.  When the glove hit the dry leaves at my feet the deer stopped on a hill about a hundred yards away.  After a moment of silence, they began running towards me again.  It was then that I could see that it was a doe that was being followed by a buck.  When the two deer got about 50 yards from me they stopped again.  I waited until they started off again and then swung the rifle over and put the scope on the buck and shot.  In the scope I could see that it looked like the buck was trying to reverse directions when it fell over.  I had shot it high in the lungs near the backbone.  It was still kicking when I approached it so I put the “coupe de grace” through its neck. 

On another hunt, probably an opening weekend since that was the only time we hunted down near Neilsville, I was sitting on Buzz’s stand when a buck came out along the trail that was running from east to west.  It stopped in a shallow depression that almost covered up the buck except for its chest and back.  I put the crosshairs of the scope where I thought the chest was and pulled the trigger.  The shot went off but the deer ran off as well. I figured out (years later) that it was probably a combination of my poor eyesight and the extra-fine crosshairs that made it very difficult to pinpoint exactly where you were shooting.
